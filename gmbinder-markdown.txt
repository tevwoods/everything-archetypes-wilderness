# Introduction

## Design Intent

We love archetypes! We knew we wanted to write a supplement that introduced new archetypes and lots of new options for existing archetypes to flesh them out and expand on the incredible ideas that have been printed in official material. What we didn't know is that Pathfinder Second Edition already has more than 100 archetypes printed in official Paizo content at the time of this writing! Because of the sheer number of them, we've divided this project into separate thematic volumes, starting with this one, Wilderness! 

Because there are already so many archetypes in official printed material, we decided to release only a single new archetype in this volume, and spend the rest of the pages focusing on expanding the archetypes that are already out there. That being said, for our new archetype we focused on creating a holistic, thorough archetype that really embraces lore, background, and storytelling potential, rather than just a new basket of feats. We hope you enjoy what we came up with: the Treespeaker!

When we thought of Wilderness, we thought about the various archetypes that have been published that thrive outside the bounds of the city. These archetypes typically spend their time out in the wild, honing their skills to make themselves unmatched trackers, gathering encyclopedic knowledge of nearby cultures, or hunting potent herbs to heal their allies and give them an edge in battle.

With that in mind, we chose the following archetypes to expand upon in this supplement: Animal Trainer, Bounty Hunter, Folklorist, Game Hunter, Herbalist, Horizon Walker, and Scout.

Some of these archetypes could potentially have a ton of crossover in what they can do because their themes are very similar in places.  The last thing we want to do is wash out their uniqueness by mixing and matching feats across these different archetypes, even though in some cases they already share a number of feats. In order to avoid that crossover, we set about carving out a unique niche for each archetype. 

You'll find more description of the niches that we chose and why we chose them at the start of each expanded archetype, but let's use Animal Trainer as a example to explain our reasoning. On the surface, the Animal Trainer is like any other archetype that grants an animal companion such as Beastmaster or Cavalier. Rather than simply grant the archetype generic animal companion feats, we looked for what it was that really made the Animal Trainer stand out. 

Because the animal companion becomes trained in Performance instead of one of their normal skills, and because the Animal Trainer has access to a powerful improvement to Commanding an Animal with Insistent Command, we thought it would be a fantastic niche for the Animal Trainer to focus around teaching their animal companion special "tricks." It's clear from the much slower progression of the Animal Trainer's companion (mature at 6th level and nimble/savage at 12th level, as opposed to mature at 4th level and incredible at 8th level for the Beastmaster) that this companion isn't necessarily meant to be a combat-oriented sidekick, though it can still be helpful in that realm. Because of that we wanted to focus in on providing more supportive aspects with the companion's new tricks.

As always, we approached this with the specific goal that we do not want to make anything that is inherently stronger than what can be found in official content. We want GMs to feel comfortable incorporating our entire supplement into their games without fear of something hidden within these pages that introduces power creep into their game. Like most people, we love the tight balance of Pathfinder Second Edition and how it keeps things simple for players and GMs alike.

That being said, the core reason we wanted to write this expansion is because Paizo has indicated that, at least for now, they aren't interested in making expansions like these. We know that we would love to have these archetypes fleshed out, so we decided to take up the mantle ourselves and also share our creations with the broader community. We hope you love it!

# Treespeaker Archetype

<div class="wide flavor subtitle">You have established an ineffably deep relationship with the trees of Golarion, to the point that you are not just their advocate and friend, but also a hand for their purposes. You possess the ability to speak to trees, but even further you have also been granted the authority to speak and act on their behalf. All Treespeakers are called to service. Some were already deeply intertwined with nature as druids or rangers, but many city-dwellers also feel the Call and choose to answer it. The ancient wisdom of the forest understands that having representatives from all walks of life is the surest way to preserve their future.</div>

<div class='wide widepage'> 

<div class="widebar">

<div class="tabified">

### DURING COMBAT ENCOUNTERS...
You draw on arboreal power to defeat enemies and protect trees and their habitats. You may cast spells, or embrace the wilder magics to enhance your body directly, or both. In either case, you fuse these abilities with those granted by your class to accomplish the will of the forest.

### DURING SOCIAL ENCOUNTERS...
Your class may determine more of how you approach social encounters, but being a Treespeaker you always have a mind toward the goals and needs of nearby forests and elder trees. Additionally, not all trees grow in the wild.  Many cities and urban areas keep trees within the city walls, and you are the defender of these as well.

### WHILE EXPLORING...
Your ability to speak with trees is utterly invaluable whenever you and your party are traveling in an area where they grow. When you find yourself traveling for long periods where trees do not grow, you are prone to becoming listless and despondent.

### IN DOWNTIME...
You might spend time mostly outside of any urban areas, connecting with the local arboreal inhabitants, or communing with them if you are already familiar with the area. In addition to local concerns, you might view this as an opportunity to tap into the ancient wisdom of the local tree network.

### YOU MIGHT...

<div class="main-bullets">

- Take a very long view of time and how events impact you and those around you.
- Have a love of nature and avoid urban areas as much as possible.
- Come from an urban background, but have given up many of your urban habits to answer the call to become a Treespeaker.

</div>

### OTHERS PROBABLY...

<div class="main-bullets">

- Are curious or fearful of your unique relationship with trees.
- May not necessarily know about your role as a Treespeaker. Not all Treespeakers make their powers or position known externally.
- Ask you for help with any question regarding nature, even if it's not something you're familiar with.

</div>

</div>


</div>

<div class="thinbar rule">

### THE CALL

All Treespeakers are called to service, but this Call can come in different forms, and is tailored to the individual being called. No one really knows who or what is responsible for the Call, though some hypothesize that it comes from Gozreh or even one of the Eldest that takes an interest in the material plane, such as the Green Mother.

Whichever form the Call takes, if the person chooses to answer it they are led to an elder tree to commune. This communion is how they gain their powers and an understanding of their new role.

### THE ROLE

Usually a Treespeaker is called to fulfill a specific need, such as trying to stop an urban expansion that threatens a forest, or to stop a group of people who threaten the stability of a nearby ecosystem. Succeed or fail, once the situation is resolved the Treespeaker retains their role and powers. 

Due to this, most Treespeakers do not have specific tasks to complete on behalf of trees for most of their service, but they stand ever ready to answer if an elder tree calls for aid.

</div></div>

# Treespeaker Feats

<div class="wide">

<p class="wide">
The treespeaker archetype allows a character to take on specific aspects of trees and arboreals to embrace the fantasy of being a tree warrior. The feats available in this archetype are divided roughly into three types: feats that give the character a very limited selection of tree-oriented spells, feats that give the character physical properties of trees that help them become better warriors, and thematic and utility feats that flesh out additional abilities that a tree mystic/warrior might have. There is, of course, nothing stopping a character from dipping into all three to blend these abilities to become the ultimate arboreal warrior mystic!
</p>

#### Treespeaker Dedication <div class="label">FEAT 6</div>
<div class="taglist">
<div class="tag uncommon">UNCOMMON</div>
<div class="tag">ARCHETYPE</div>
<div class="tag">DEDICATION</div>
</div>
<div class="rule">
You have been called to become a Treespeaker. Answering this Call and receiving the benefits of this dedication feat usually requires you to commune with an elder tree for one week to receive your powers and instructions, though exceptions are sometimes made for extreme situations, such as when you are very far from the nearest elder tree, or unable to reach one for another reason. Exceptions are also made if the reason of the call is urgent enough that a one week communion would prevent you from fulfilling your calling. Regardless, you must return to the elder tree to complete your communion within one year of your calling, or you lose all benefits of this archetype. 

After you have completed your communion, you learn the arboreal language, which is spoken by most awakened trees. You must still use <i>speak with plants</i> to communicate with trees that are not awakened or do not understand arboreal.

You gain the ability to cast *one with plants* at will. If you cast *one with plants* near other trees, such that your root systems intertwine, you may spend 1 hour to gain vague impressions of what the nearby trees have experienced over the past 24 hours.

Once per day, you may cast *speak with plants*, except that it only works with trees. Most trees have a vague awareness of the actions of creatures around them, unless those creatures are actively threatening nearby trees. Some trees do take a particular interest in the affairs of creatures, however, and it is up to your GM to determine what kind of trees you encounter.

<b>Special</b> You can't select another dedication feat until you have gained two other feats from the Treespeaker archetype.

##### THE NATURE OF TREES

Golarion's trees come in a number of varieties, not just in their shape, color, size, leafage and fruit, but also in their level of consciousness and intelligence. Many trees have only a vague impression of the events around them, gained mostly through the collective of their root system, and limited intelligence to interpret that information.

But there are a great many trees that are very intelligent and possess their own personalities and interests, apart from the collective. In addition to these, there are many arboreals: humanoid tree creatures that nurture and defend forests.

# Animal Trainer

<div class="wide">

<p>
<b>Originally Printed</b> Pathfinder #152: Legacy of the Lost God
</p>

<p class="wide">
Animal Trainer was originally printed in the second book of Extinction Curse, which is an adventure path that revolves around a traveling circus and the characters that work in and around that circus. In that context, even though Animal Trainer has a lot in common mechanically with Beastmaster and other archetypes that grant an animal companion, it's clear that Animal Trainer is meant to be more geared toward the "lion  jumping through a hoop of fire" style of animal companion.  Unfortunately the original printing didn't allow a lot of space to expand on the theme, with only two feats that stepped outside the core animal companion progression, and a minor deviation in which skills your animal companion gets access to.

With that in mind, we focused our expansion of the archetype on the tricks that you and your animal companion are able to pull off when you work together. Because the animal trainer's animal companion lags behind the others in how their raw numbers advance, we wanted to focus these tricks around utility and being helpful to the animal trainer and their allies in and out of combat.
</p>

# Bounty Hunter

<div class="wide">

<p>
<b>Originally Printed</b> Advanced Player's Guide
</p>

<p class="wide">
Bounty Hunter shares some similarities to the ranger, such as a Hunt Prey action that brings in a more social aspect by giving a bonus to Gather Information checks regarding your prey, and allowing you to hunt a particular prey off of something like a bounty board or wanted poster. This is at the same time more freeing and more restrictive than the ranger's Hunt Prey, since they are not able to hunt a creature they're Tracking, but instead they can Hunt Prey just by hearing <i>about</i> a particular target.

There is an emphasis on non-lethal damage to subdue a target and ostensibly bring them to justice, and even further incorporation of a social aspect from its Posse feat, which has the potential to give your entire party bonuses related to your Hunt Prey action. In expanding this archetype, we focused on three core elements: exploring the social aspects of hunting a target, honing in on non-lethal takedowns to capture a target rather than kill them, and keeping a target in your sights once you've found them, in the vein of Keep Pace and Opportunistic Grapple.

We have also added three new weapons that are especially suitable for bounty hunters: the lasso, the barbed lasso, and the net gun (page {page-start-gear}). The net gun has its own proficiency feat listed here, but we recommend adding the lasso and barbed lasso to the list of weapons you gain training with in the Tools of the Trade feat (APG).
</p>

# Folklorist

<div class="wide">

<p>
<b>Originally Printed</b> Pathfinder #170: Spoken on the Song Wind
</p>

<p class="wide">
Folklorist takes a lot of its design and inspiration from the Bard class. Its core action Spin Tale, granted by the dedication feat, is similar to the Bard's Inspire Courage ability, and its Folktales Lore strongly parallels the Bard's Bardic Lore. Apart from these similarities the Folklorist distinguishes itself with its focus on carrying and sharing lore and telling stories as a form of oral history.

It makes sense to expand upon those themes, focusing on sharing lore and history with others, both in a utility sense and in a way that empowers allies by inspiring them to heroic acts. In the vein of Rule of Three, we have also expanded the archetype with options that revolve around how the story unfolds, giving the folklorist a unique way to engage with each encounter.

Because of the fact that you cannot declare yourself to be the hero of Spin Tale, the Folklorist is clearly meant to be an archetype centered on empowering your friends and allies. We lean heavily into that theme to make Folklorist a viable route for any character that wants to have a strong supporting role in the party.
</p>

# Game Hunter

<div class="wide">

<p>
<b>Originally Printed</b> Pathfinder #175: Broken Tusk Moon
</p>

<p class="wide">
Game Hunter takes a lot of inspiration from the Ranger and Bounty Hunter skill set, but incorporates an element of honor and respect toward the hunted. Because its Hunt Prey action is limited to the types of creatures it can affect, this archetype has a tendency to be hit-or-miss. Often these creature types might feature strongly in a certain part of an adventure, but later there is a tonal or regional shift and those creatures don't appear nearly as often, or at all.  This can create vestigial player selections: features that are part of your character, but no longer really function. You may not want to retrain them because they represent something fundamental about who your character is or where they came from, but you also gain very little benefit from them.

We added It's All Game to give players an option to overcome this problem by unrestricting their Hunt Prey ability. Because the Game Hunter's Hunt Prey action receives additional benefits beyond the Ranger's, it makes sense to spend a class feat to unlock this power for all creature types.

With that in mind, we also want to really focus in on the way that a Game Hunter honors their fallen foes and the unique way they treat their prey, since this is the core distinction between it and the Bounty Hunter, which is otherwise very similar mechanically.
</p>

# Horizon Walker

<div class="wide">

<p>
<b>Originally Printed</b> Advanced Player's Guide
</p>

<p class="wide">
The Horizon Walker archetype is about becoming the absolute master over a type of wilderness of your choosing. Finding safe paths through your favored terrain and guiding your allies through that same terrain is the core concept of the archetype. 

Inherently since this archetype focuses on travel through extreme terrains, there is an emphasis on non-combat feats and abilities. This puts the Horizon Walker into a fairly narrow niche: when you're traveling through the rougher parts of your favored terrain, your abilities can be quite useful, however most campaigns don't have much emphasis on these types of difficulties. 

This means the Horizon Walker is a binary choice: if the campaign has a lot of environmental hazards in a specific type of terrain, then Horizon Walker is incredibly powerful, likely single-handedly bypassing many of the challenges of those parts of the campaign. If the campaign does not feature that kind of situation often or at all, then the Horizon Walker is relegated to a more flavor-based choice, and adds very little to the character's potential in terms of mechanics.

To overcome these limitations, we focus on two core aspects: broadening the Horizon Walker's potential usefulness in a campaign that crosses multiple types of terrain, and making them the masters of their favored terrain, rather than just masters of traveling through it. Versatile Horizons allows the Horizon Walker to expand their favored terrain selection, which is perfect for campaigns that start primarily in one biome but then transition to another. It only makes sense that the Horizon Walker would quickly become a master of their new terrain without forgetting what they know of the old one. In terms of making Horizon Walkers masters of their terrain, we added feats that let them Recall Knowledge about creatures in their favored terrain, spot hazards, create hazards, and expand upon their magical adaptations. Previous iterations of the Horizon Walker have also seen them mastering travel of other planes in addition to the material plane, so we have recaptured some of that essence as well.
</p>

# Scout

<div class="wide">

<p>
<b>Originally Printed</b> Advanced Player's Guide
</p>

<p class="wide">
The Scout archetype revolves around three core elements: seeing, moving, and not being seen. Usually ranging ahead of and behind their allies, the scout attempts to keep their party from being surprised, and if possible manufactures ways to surprise any enemies they are able to scout out ahead of time.

The core scout feats revolve around these concepts, gaining feats that allow them to move around, hide, sneak, and attack. We lean into the scout's ability to ambush enemies, move around the field of combat, and also add interesting feats that embrace ranged combat styles since the core archetype includes mostly support for melee combat styles.

## Weapons

<p>
<b>Net gun</b> The net gun is powered by black powder charges to launch a folded net with weighted ends at an enemy to entangle them. The net is attached to two lead ropes that anchor back to the firearm. Once a net is fired, you can quickly grab these lead ropes with one hand and use them to keep the target grappled.  Packing and loading the net is a difficult and time-consuming task, so net guns are often only fired once in a given engagement, though it is said that some people have mastered the art of reloading them quickly. Ammunition for a net gun is a pre-packed net charge that costs 1gp each. However, you may recover any spent charges as a 10 minute activity to repair and repack the nets.
</p>

<p>
<b>Lasso</b> The lasso is a treated rope tied in a specific way to allow it to be thrown over some part of a creature and pulled tight.
</p>

<p>
<b>Barbed lasso</b> The barbed lasso is a variation on the lasso in which metal barbs are woven into the end of the rope to make it a particularly brutal way of capturing someone.
</p>

## Weapon Traits

<p>
<b>Ranged grapple</b> A weapon with the ranged grapple trait always has a lead: some kind of rope, chain, or wire. This lead is used to initiate a grapple and maintain it. You may attempt an Athletics check to Grapple a target that is within the first range increment of the weapon, and you may attempt to maintain that Grapple on subsequent turns as long as you still have the lead in one hand. If a creature that is grabbed or restrained this way is ever beyond the first range increment of the weapon, the grabbed or restrained condition ends. If an effect would cause a grabbed or restrained creature to be forcefully moved outside of this range, you may attempt an Athletics check against the initiating creature's Fortitude DC or spell DC as appropriate to prevent the forced movement. You do not need a free hand to Grapple when using a weapon with this trait, but you do have to continue to hold the lead. If a weapon with this trait is two-handed, changing grip so that you are carrying the weapon in one hand and holding the lead is a free action. Similarly dropping the lead and resuming two-handed use of the weapon is a free action. If you Release the lead, the Grapple ends and the target is no longer grabbed or restrained by you. Unless otherwise specified, you cannot attack with this weapon while you have a target grappled with it. 
</p>

## Ammunition

<p>
<b>Bag rounds</b> These small bags filled with soft pellets can be shot from a firearm instead of normal rounds. These rounds can only deal bludgeoning damage, even if fired from a concussive weapon, and you do not take a penalty when attempting a non-lethal attack when using these rounds. You can attempt to make a lethal attack with these rounds with a -2 circumstance penalty. Purchased in sets of 10.
</p>

<p>
<b>Blunted arrows/bolts</b> Instead of an arrow or bolt for puncturing or making large wounds, these shafts end in a blunt tip. When fired from a weapon in the bow group the weapon deals bludgeoning damage, and you do not take a penalty when attempting a non-lethal attack when using this ammunition. You can attempt to make a lethal attack with this ammunition with a -2 circumstance penalty. Purchased in sets of 10.
</p>
